Hypothesis testing for difference in proportions¶

Two sample Z-test of proportions¶

Example¶

Time magazine reported the result of a telephone poll of 800 adult Americans. The question posed of the Americans who were surveyed was: "Should the federal tax on cigarettes be raised to pay for health care reform?" The results of the survey were:

  • 351 out of 605 non-smokers said "yes"
  • 41 out of 195 smokers said "yes"

Is there sufficient evidence at the $\alpha = 0.05$ to conclude that the two populations differ significantly with respect to their opinions?

Let $p_1$ = the proportion of the non-smoker population who reply "yes" and $p_2$ = the proportion of the smoker population who reply "yes"

Example from: https://online.stat.psu.edu/stat415/lesson/9/9.4 Pennsylvania State University

Calculate proportions¶

Non-smokers: $\hat{p}_1 = \frac{351}{605} = 0.58$

Smokers: $\hat{p}_2 = \frac{41}{195} = 0.21$

Overall sample proportion: $\hat{p} = \frac{41 + 351}{195 + 605} = 0.49$

Null model¶

$$H_0:p_1 = p_2 \;$$

If the sample is taken at random, independent and $np\ge10$ and $n(1-p) \ge 10$, then

$$\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2 \sim N\bigg(0, \sqrt{p(1-p) \big(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}}\big) \bigg)$$

Step 1: Set up the hypotheses¶

$$H_0:p_1 = p_2 \; vs \; H_1: p_1 \ne p_2$$

Step 2: Calculate the test statistic¶

$$ Z = \frac{(0.58 - 0.21) - 0 }{\sqrt{0.49 (0.51)( \frac{1}{195} + \frac{1}{605})}} = 8.99$$

Step 3: Null model¶

$$Z\sim N(0, 1)$$

Step 4: P-value¶

The P-value is less than 0.05, therefore we reject the null hypothesis. There is sufficient evidence at the 0.05 level to conclude that the two populations differ with respect to their opinions concerning imposing a federal tax to help pay for health care reform.

Hypothesis testing for one mean¶

Recall: Sampling distribution of the sample mean¶

Let $x_1, x_2, ..., x_n$ be independent values of a random sample from some population.

For a sufficiently large sample size $n$ and with other necessary conditions then: $$\bar{X} \sim N(\mu, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}})$$ approx

Note: If the population distribution is normal, the sample mean $\bar{X}$ follows the Normal model with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$ regardless of the sample size.

Null distribution¶

Null hypothesis: statement about the population parameter

$$H_0: \mu = \mu_0$$

where $\mu_0$ is some value

Assuming $\mu = \mu_0$ is true, for a sufficiently large sample size $n$ and with other necessary conditions:

$$\bar{X} \sim N(\mu_0, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}})$$ approximately

Test statistic¶

Case 1: If $\sigma$ is known¶

The test statistic is the z-score for the observed sample mean under the assumption that the null hypothesis is true

$$Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}$$

Case 2: If $\sigma$ is unknown¶

  • We will estimate $\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$ with $\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$

$t$ - Distribution¶

  • $t$-distribution is bell shaped, unimodal and symmetric about 0
  • Probabilities depend on the degrees of freedom, $df = n-1$
  • the $t_{n-1}$ has longer tails than the normal
  • $n \rightarrow \infty$, $t_{n-1} \rightarrow Z \;\sim \;N (0, 1)$
  • $t-$distribution has thicker tails and is more spread out than the standard normal distribution

image source: Geeksforgeeks.org

  • Test statistic:

$$t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}} $$

will follow a t-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom.

Assumptions and conditions¶

  • The sample is randomly drawn from the population.
  • The sample values are independent. In general, if your sample size is greater than 10% of the population size, there will be a severe violation of independence.
  • Normality:
    • For proportions:
      • Check $n\times p_0 \ge 10$ and $n\times(1-p_0) \ge 10$
    • For means:
      • When the underlying distribution of $x$ is non-Normal or unknown, sample size must be large enough
      • When the underlying distribution of $x$ is exactly or nearly Normal, using the t-model is justified with small sample sizes

Recall: Confidence Interval for Population mean $\mu$ is¶

Previous module we said the confidence interval for $\mu$ is: $$\bar{x} \pm z^* \times \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

  • For large $n$, the standard normal and $t$-distributions are quite close and we could use

$$\bar{x} \pm z^* \times \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$$

  • In practice when we don't know the population standard deviation $\sigma$
  • In practice, we estimate $\sigma$ by the sample standard deviation $s$
  • We replace the $Z$-score by the slightly larger score $t$-score

$$\bar{x} \pm t^{*}_{n-1} \times \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$$

Function in R for the t Distribution¶

  • We can use software packages to get the desired probability or quantiles.

  • Probabilities:

pt(q, df)

  • Quantiles:

Two sample t test for independent groups¶

Step 1: Set up the hypotheses¶

$H_0: \mu_1-\mu_2 = \Delta_0 $ vs $ H_1: \mu_1-\mu_2 < \Delta_0$ $\qquad$ or $\qquad$ $H_0: \mu_1-\mu_2 = \Delta_0 $ vs $ H_1: \mu_1-\mu_2 > \Delta_0$ $\qquad$ or $\qquad$ $H_0: \mu_1-\mu_2 = \Delta_0 $ vs $ H_1: \mu_1-\mu_2 \neq \Delta_0$ $\qquad$

where $\Delta_0$ is the hypothesized value of the population mean.

Step 2: Calculate the test statistic¶

$$ T = \frac{\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2 - \Delta_0}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}$$

Step 3: Null model¶

$$T \sim t_\nu $$ where the degrees of freedom $\nu = min(n_1 - 1, n_2 - 1)$. Note in the worksheet you will see another approximation of $\nu$ in the worksheet.

Step 4 and 5: P-value and Conclusion¶

Same process as before

Assumptions and conditions for validity of using the t-model:¶

  1. The two samples are randomly drawn from their respective populations.
  2. The sampled individuals within the same sample are independent of each other. It suffices to check that two sample sizes are no greater than 10% of their respective population sizes.
  • If both $x_1$ and $x_2$ follow the Normal model, there is no restriction on the sample sizes $n_1$ and $n_2$.
  • If $x_1$ and $x_2$ are non-Normal or follow an unknown distribution, we need reasonably large sample sizes to validate the Normal approximation by the CLT as well as the use of the t-model.
  1. The two samples must be independent of each other.

Paired data¶

Paired t-test¶

  • $d_i$ = within-pair differences

  • $\bar{d}$ = mean of the within pair differences

  • $s_d$ = standard deviation of the within pair differences

Step 1: Set up the hypotheses¶

$H_0: \mu_d = \Delta_0 $ vs $ H_1: \mu_d < \Delta_0$ $\qquad$ or $\qquad$ $H_0:\mu_d = \Delta_0 $ vs $ H_1: \mu_d> \Delta_0$ $\qquad$ or $\qquad$ $H_0: \mu_d = \Delta_0 $ vs $ H_1: \mu_d \neq \Delta_0$

where $\mu_d$ is the population mean difference and $\Delta_0$ is the hypothesized value of the population mean.

Step 2: Calculate the test statistic¶

$$ T = \frac{\bar{d} - \Delta_0 }{{\frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}}}}$$

Step 3: Null model¶

$$T \sim t_{n-1} $$ where $n$ represents the number of pairs.

Step 4 and 5: P-value and Conclusion¶

Same process as before

Confidence intervals for the mean difference between two paired groups¶

$$\bar{d} \pm t^{*}_{n-1} \times \frac{s_n}{\sqrt{n}}$$

where $n$ represents the number of pairs

Assumptions and conditions for validity of using the t-model:¶

  1. The n pairs are randomly drawn from the population.
  2. The n pairs are independent of each other, i.e. any two distinct pairs are independent of each other. It suffices to check that the number of pairs $n$ is no greater than 10% of the population size.
  • If $d_i$’s follow the Normal model, there is no restriction on the sample size n.
  • If $d_i$’s follow a nearly Normal distribution (symmetric and unimodal), it will be fine even if the sample size $n$ is small.
  • If $d_i$’s follow an unknown or a non-Normal distribution, we need a reasonably large sample size $n$ to validate the Normal approximation of $\bar{d}$ by the CLT as well as the use of the t-model.

Check also the following are satisfied:

  1. The population standard deviation of the differences $d_i$’s, $\sigma_d$, is unknown.
  2. The data must be paired.

Bootstrapping vs. theory based approaches¶

Traditional theory based approach¶

  • Require particular assumptions about the distribution
    • results may not be valid if the assumptions are are not met
  • Uses theory to tell what the sampling distribution should look like. We use equations to estimate sampling distribution for specific sample statistics

Bootstrapping approach¶

  • Does not make assumption about the distribution
    • If sample does a reasonable job of representing the population then bootstrap will work well (e.g. for small sample sizes, the sample may not do a good job of representing the population)
  • Bootstrap is useful for cases where formula for sample statistics do not exist
