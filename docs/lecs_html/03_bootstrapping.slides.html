Notes¶

  • We have a population (UBC students) and we want to estimate a population parameter (e.g, mean GPA)
  • We take a sample of size n and calculate the point estimate (e.g., sample mean)
  • Our point estimate is almost never the same as the population parameter due to random chance
  • Imagine we repeat this process multiple times to get a sampling distribution of point estimates
  • The sampling distribution gives us all the possible values of point estimates and the frequency of occurence
  • We can quantify the uncertainty of our estimates by computing the standard deviation of sample means, also called standard error
  • The sampling distribution has some nice properties:
    • It centers around the population parameter
    • With sufficiently large n, the sampling distribution follow a bell-shaped curve (i.e., CLT)
    • If the sampling distribution is bell-shaped, then 95% of the estimates fall within 2 SE of the population parameter (we will revisit this later in week 7)

Summary of what we've learned so far¶

A central goal of this course: Estimate a population parameter, along with how certain you are.

The sampling distribution shows us:

  1. What point estimates are possible (even more: their probabilities of occurring, too)
  2. Where the true parameter is (in most cases, lies at the mean of the sampling distribution)

True

Motivation for Today's Topic¶

  • The sampling distribution is never known in practice, just as the population parameter is unknown.

    • Sometimes we don't have the resources to collect infinite number of samples. We can have access to a single sample
    • There are different sample statistics (e.g., percentile, median, variance) where the underlying sampling distribution is unknown (i.e., cannot apply CLT)

  • If we knew the sampling distribution, there would be no need to estimate the population parameter. Why?

  • Our goal: to estimate the sampling distribution, and interpret it differently from the actual sampling distribution.

What is (non-parametric) bootstrapping?¶

(non-parametric) Bootstrapping is a type of resampling where samples of the same size are repeatedly drawn, with replacement, from a single original sample

Question for you: Why don't we just resample without replacement?

  • Resampling without replacement will just return the same original sample
  • Resampling with replacement to mimic the sampling variation

population <- tibble(student = 1:10000, grade = rnorm(n = 10000, mean = 70, sd = 10))

samples <- rep_sample_n(population, size= 200, replace=FALSE, reps =3000)
sampling_dist <- samples %>% group_by(replicate) %>% summarise(mean_grade = mean(grade))

single_sample <- rep_sample_n(population, size= 200, replace=FALSE)
bootstrap_samples <- rep_sample_n(single_sample, size= nrow(single_sample), replace=TRUE, reps =3000)
bootstrap_dist <- bootstrap_samples %>% group_by(replicate) %>% summarise(mean_grade = mean(grade))

Notes¶

  • Sampling distribution centers around the population parameter
  • Bootstrap distribution centers around the sample mean
  • Even though the centers of bootstrap distribution and sampling distribution, the bootstrap standard error is a good estimate of the sampling standard error

Why bootstrap is good¶

  • Apply to many sample statistics (means, proportions, median, percentile)
  • Works even when there is no ready formula for a standard error (i.e., sampling distribution is unknown)
  • It doesn't require a normally distributed data

Why bootstrap is not good¶

  • If the sample is not representative, the boostrap distribution will be biased
  • Does not work well when the original sample size is small
